First time pitching? A pitch deck is usually 10–15 slides that tells investors the story of your business: what problem exists, how you solve it, how big the market is, how much money you've made, and why your team can win. Think of it like a well-designed magazine spread — visual, punchy, and leaving them wanting to know more. Your goal is NOT to share every detail. It's to earn a second meeting.

Cover Slide
Your first impression. Make it unmistakably ButterBawd.
~5 seconds to hook them
👁️ What the investor sees
Before you say a word, this slide is projected. It should communicate: who you are, what you do, and what vibe you carry. A strong cover creates curiosity. A weak one creates skepticism before you've even started.
💡 Suggestion: Keep it to 10–15 words. Focus on who you serve and what makes you different. Avoid jargon like "disrupting" or "revolutionizing."
🚩 Common mistake
Don't put your funding ask, financial projections, or revenue numbers on the cover slide. Keep this slide clean, confident, and visual. One logo, one line, one name.

The Problem
Make investors feel the frustration your customer feels every day.
Make them feel it
👁️ What the investor needs to understand
A great problem slide creates an "aha!" moment. They should think: "Oh, I know someone who has this exact problem." For ButterBawd, the problem is very real, very specific, and very underserved.
💡 Use real data or emotions. Example: "We surveyed 200 Indian women — 78% said they've stopped wearing shapewear in summer because it's unbearable." If you have any numbers, add them here.
🚩 Common mistake
Don't make the problem too broad ("fashion is a big industry"). The more specific and emotional your problem, the more credible your solution. Investors fund solutions to real, urgent, painful problems.

Slide 04 / 12
Market Size
Prove this is a massive, growing, and winnable opportunity.
Show the size of the prize
👁️ What the investor needs to believe
Investors need to believe this market is big enough to build a large business. They also want to see you're not chasing all of it — just the realistic slice (SOM) that you can own in the next 3–5 years. Learn these three terms: TAM → SAM → SOM.
TAM = Total Addressable Market (the entire industry). SAM = Serviceable Addressable Market (the part you can reach). SOM = Serviceable Obtainable Market (the part you'll actually win). Always go TAM → SAM → SOM, from big to small.

Slide 06 / 12
Traction
Proof that real people love and buy ButterBawd — even early signals matter.
Your most important slide
👁️ What the investor is asking themselves
Has anyone paid money for this? Do customers come back? Is there organic buzz, or is every order bought through ads? Traction is the single biggest filter — even early, imperfect traction shows that the market is real.
⚠️ Be honest here. Never inflate numbers. Investors do due diligence — they will find out. But also: don't undersell real wins. 8,300 Instagram followers with 108 curated posts, media features in Elle India, and a viral product IS meaningful traction for a brand launched in 2025.
💡 Screenshot DMs, Shopify reviews, or WhatsApp messages from happy customers. These emotional proof points are often more persuasive than charts.
Slide 07 / 12
Business Model
How does ButterBawd make money — and how does that improve over time?
Show you understand margins
👁️ What the investor is calculating in their head
Revenue is nice. Margin is everything. They want to know: What does it cost to make a garment? What do you sell it for? What's left after shipping, payment gateway, returns, and marketing? That remaining number — contribution margin — is what they care about most.
Contribution Margin formula: Selling Price − COGS − Shipping − Payment Gateway − Returns − Marketing = Contribution Margin. A healthy D2C brand aims for 40–60%+ contribution margin.
💡 Even if you don't have all numbers finalized, showing that you understand unit economics and can articulate the path to profitability builds enormous investor confidence.
Slide 08 / 12
Competitive Positioning
Show exactly where ButterBawd wins — and why you can't be easily copied.
Know your battlefield
👁️ The question investors are really asking
If you say "we have no competition," you've lost the room. Every sophisticated investor knows there's always competition. Acknowledging competitors and clearly articulating your advantage shows maturity and strategic clarity.
Slide 09 / 12
Go-To-Market Strategy
How do you acquire customers — and keep them coming back?
The growth engine
👁️ What investors are assessing
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) is the biggest cost for most D2C brands. Investors want to see a brand that acquires customers efficiently and keeps them (retention). Organic channels (content, community, PR) are gold — they want to see you're not dependent on paid ads alone.
Slide 10 / 12
The Team
Why is Ritika Chowdhury the right person to build this brand?
They bet on people, not plans
👁️ The single most important question investors ask
"Why YOU?" Investors know that ideas change, markets shift, and plans evolve — but the right founder finds a way. Tell your story: what lived experience, skill, and obsession makes you the only person who could build ButterBawd?
💡 Even for a solo founder, showing AWARENESS of what gaps exist and a plan to fill them (with funding) is a strong signal. "I know what I don't know" is reassuring to investors.
Slide 11 / 12
Financial Projections
Show your path to profitability — not just big, unbelievable numbers.
Be realistic, be directional
👁️ What investors expect at Seed stage
At seed stage, nobody expects perfect 5-year projections. What they DO expect: that you understand your unit economics, have a believable growth model, and know how long your runway is. They'll pressure-test your assumptions — so build them with logic, not hope.
🚩 DO NOT: Put ₹100Cr revenue in Year 3 without a clear path. Investors will dismiss it immediately. Instead, show a bottom-up model: "If we sell X units at ₹Y average price via Z channels, we reach revenue W."
Slide 12 / 12
The Ask
Ask confidently. Know exactly what you need and why.
Close with confidence
👁️ What the investor is listening for
The ask should feel earned by everything that came before. You've proven the problem, the solution, the market, the traction — now ask for what you need to get to the next stage. Be specific. Know your number, your runway, and what milestones you'll hit.
💡 Common seed stage range for Indian D2C in 2025: ₹50L – ₹3Cr. The exact number should reflect 12–18 months of runway + specific milestone targets. Don't ask for too little (signals lack of ambition) or too much (signals lack of discipline).
💡 Consult a CA or startup advisor before deciding on valuation. First-time founders often either undervalue (give too much equity) or overvalue (unrealistic expectations). Research comparable seed deals in Indian D2C fashion.

Your Elevator Pitch — 3 Versions
What is an elevator pitch? Imagine you step into an elevator with an investor. You have 30 seconds before they get off. Can you make them want to know more? Practice all three versions — you never know which situation you'll be in.
⚡ 30-Second Version
30 sec | ~75 words
"ButterBawd is India's first sculpting essentials brand engineered for our weather and our bodies. We make compression tops and bodysuits using our own proprietary fabrics — breathable shapewear that actually works in 35-degree Mumbai humidity. We've already been featured in Elle India, built a community of 8,000+ on Instagram, and our hero product went viral organically. We're raising a seed round now to scale inventory and expand to Nykaa. I'm Ritika — want to see the deck?"
🎯 2-Minute Version
2 min | ~250 words
"I want to tell you about a problem every Indian woman knows — you buy shapewear because you want to look snatched. But within an hour in Indian weather, you're overheated, uncomfortable, and peeling it off in a bathroom.

I started ButterBawd because I couldn't find a single sculpting essential that was designed for us — Indian bodies, Indian climate, Indian aspirations.

So we built our own fabrics. Three of them. SoftServe™ for everyday wear, RibCloud™ for structure, and SkinMelt™ — our signature 76% Polyamide blend that gives real sculpting without suffocating you. We manufacture in-house in India. That means quality control, faster iteration, and margins that improve as we scale.

In a few months, we've built 8,300 organic Instagram followers, earned coverage in Elle India and The New Indian Express, and our Heartline Melt Top went viral without a single rupee in paid ads.

The Indian shapewear and athleisure market is $700M and growing. No brand owns the premium, aesthetic, climate-specific D2C space — that's exactly where we sit.

We're raising ₹[X] Cr to expand inventory, scale our Nykaa presence, and build the team. I'd love to share more — do you have 20 minutes for a proper conversation?"
📊 5-Minute Full Pitch Opener
5 min | ~600 words
"I want to start with a question: How many of you know a woman who's worn shapewear and then taken it off halfway through an event because it was just too hot?

[Pause. Let them nod.]

That's our customer. And that's our problem.

My name is Ritika Chowdhury, and I'm the founder of ButterBawd — India's first sculpting essentials brand built specifically for Indian weather, Indian bodies, and the modern Indian woman's wardrobe.

The global shapewear market is dominated by brands like SKIMS and Spanx — designed for Western climates, Western bodies, and Western price expectations. Locally, we have Zivame and PrettySecrets — but they're intimate wear brands, not body-sculpting lifestyle labels. And the fast fashion players? They use cheap materials, poor construction, and zero fabric science.

The result: there is no premium, breathable, aesthetically cohesive sculpting brand in India. We're building that brand.

ButterBawd's product is built on three proprietary fabric innovations — SoftServe, RibCloud, and SkinMelt. These fabrics are engineered from scratch in our in-house facility. SkinMelt, our hero — 76% Polyamide, 24% Spandex — delivers genuine shapewear-level compression while remaining breathable in 35-degree heat. You can wear it all day. You actually want to.

We've designed for confidence, not correction. Double-lined for bra-free wearability. Colorways that feel elevated: Cocoa Whip, Sugar Blush, Spiced Butter. Products that look as good on your Instagram as they do in person.

Since launching, we've built 8,300 organic followers, earned editorial coverage in Elle India and The New Indian Express, and our Heartline Melt Top went viral — all without paid advertising. Our Shopify store runs on Gokwik's checkout infrastructure, and our community is growing with zero influencer spend.

The market we're addressing is $700M in India alone, growing at 8–10% a year. BlissClub — our closest comparable — just reported 51% revenue growth in FY25. The wave is real. The timing is now.

We're raising ₹[X] Cr to scale inventory, expand to Nykaa Fashion, and hire our core operations team. I know exactly what this capital will unlock: [specific milestones].

ButterBawd is not a fashion brand that makes bodies — it's an infrastructure brand that makes Indian women feel powerful in their own skin. I'd love your partnership in building it."

👤 Understanding Investor Types
👼
Angel Investor
Pre-Seed → Seed

Individual investors (often successful founders or executives) who invest their own money at very early stages. They're often faster to decide and take more personal bets on founders.

  • Ticket size: ₹5L – ₹50L typically
  • What they value: The founder's conviction + story
  • For ButterBawd: Ideal first money — fashion-industry angels, startup veterans
  • Where to find them: LetsVenture, AngelList India, LinkedIn, founder communities
🌱
Seed VC Fund
Seed → Series A Bridge

Specialized funds that invest at the seed stage. They have more process than angels but back earlier-stage companies. They'll do light due diligence and provide more than just money — networks, hiring help, brand credibility.

  • Ticket size: ₹50L – ₹3Cr
  • What they value: Traction signals + scalable model
  • For ButterBawd: Indian D2C focused funds (Fireside, RPSG, Titan Capital)
  • Process: Term sheet → due diligence → investment
🏛️
Strategic Investor
Any Stage

Corporate investors (like conglomerates, retail companies, or fabric manufacturers) who invest because your success benefits their business. Can be incredibly valuable beyond just money.

  • Examples: Aditya Birla Fashion, Reliance Retail ventures, fabric suppliers
  • What they bring: Distribution, supply chain, retail access
  • For ButterBawd: A fabric conglomerate investing could unlock premium raw materials + retail shelf space
  • ⚠️ Watch: Can come with conditions that limit flexibility

🌊 Key Trends Working in ButterBawd's Favour
💪
Compression → Confidence Shift
Shapewear is no longer about "correction" — it's about confidence enhancement. The language of the category is changing from restrictive/corrective to empowering/confidence-boosting. This is exactly ButterBawd's positioning.
→ ButterBawd angle: "Made for real bodies ✨" — confidence-first positioning already live on Instagram
🧘
Athleisure Goes Everyday
The line between gym wear and casual wear has dissolved. Women want one piece that works for Pilates at 8am, WFH at noon, and brunch at 2pm. Versatility is the new luxury.
→ ButterBawd angle: Double-lined, bra-free bodysuits that do exactly this — from workout to Instagram-ready
🇮🇳
Vocal for Local — D2C Brand Love
